A
Understand the business 2 topics
A1 Business understanding questions 10 questions
What exactly is the bank lending against?
Units, value, only approved schemes, only demat holdings, or both SOA and demat — this single choice reshapes custody, pledge flow, and the whole integration design.
Term loan or overdraft / credit line?
One of the biggest design splitters. A term loan disburses once as a fixed amount; an OD/line lets the customer draw as needed with interest only on the used amount. The system design diverges sharply from here.
Who controls the mutual fund holdings?
Already inside the wealth platform, discovered via RTA/CAMS/KFintech/MF Central or the depository, or manually declared by the customer. This decides trust level, eligibility checks, fraud controls, and reconciliation effort.
Do we allow all schemes?
Or only debt, hybrid, or selected equity funds — excluding ELSS, lock-ins, and lien-marked schemes. This turns into a dedicated scheme eligibility service.
What is the LTV policy?
Example: liquid fund up to 80%, debt fund 70%, equity fund 50%. Loan logic is never one rule — it is a policy engine.
What happens when NAV falls?
Alert the customer, block fresh drawdown, ask for top-up collateral, or auto-liquidate pledged units. This is the heart of risk design.
Who performs pledge marking?
Can the bank do it directly, or does it route through an RTA, BSE StarMF / NSE NMF, a registrar, or a wealth-platform partner? Skipping this question makes the rest of the design fictional.
When is money disbursed?
After eKYC, holdings fetch, eligibility, pledge confirmation, loan agreement, mandate/eSign, and fraud checks — the exact ordering of these steps matters as much as the steps themselves.
Can the customer keep transacting in the pledged folio?
Redeem unpledged units, keep SIPs running, switch schemes, continue receiving dividends? These aren't side questions — they define the entire collateral control model.
What is the source of truth for outstanding collateral value?
Yesterday's NAV, latest available NAV, an internal cached valuation, or a partner valuation feed? Get this wrong and risk management breaks underneath everything else.
A2 First-principles model mental model
Customer holds units→ Bank marks lien / pledge→ Loan up to allowed %→ If value falls → margin call / sell / liquidate
  • The system really solves five core problems, in order: identify holdings, decide eligibility, lock collateral, give and manage the loan, and continuously manage risk.

C2 Sync vs async split design splitter
Synchronous — for user-facing speed
Fetch holdings, estimate eligibility, show the offer instantly. The customer wants a fast response here.
Asynchronous — for resilience
Pledge request, partner confirmation, loan activation. External systems are slow and failure-prone, so these steps run outside the request/response cycle.
  • Design principle: build the journey as a state machine, not one long API chain.
C3 Journey state machine 13 states
INITIATED→HOLDINGS_FETCHED→ELIGIBILITY_COMPUTED→OFFER_ACCEPTED→AGREEMENT_SIGNED→PLEDGE_REQUESTED→PLEDGE_CONFIRMED→LOAN_ACCOUNT_CREATED→DISBURSED→ACTIVE→MARGIN_SHORTFALL→LIQUIDATION_IN_PROGRESS→CLOSED / FAILED
Questions a state model has to answer
  • If pledge succeeds but disbursement fails, what happens?
  • If the agreement is signed but the pledge is rejected — restart, or roll back?
  • If the customer exits the app midway, where do they resume?
  • Can the support team replay the journey safely?

D2Eligibility engineproblem 2
Excludes ELSS (lock-in), closed-ended schemes, low-liquidity / high-volatility funds, and non-approved AMC + scheme combinations.
input
scheme_id, amc_id, fund_type, lock_in_flag, volatility_bucket, regulatory_status, ops_support_flag
output
eligible / ineligible, max_ltv, risk_bucket, reason_code
Never hardcode if scheme_type == EQUITY then 50%
This logic changes often — build a policy table or rule engine instead.
D3Valuation & drawing powerproblem 3
formula
Collateral value = units × latest eligible NAV. Drawing power = Σ(collateral value × allowed LTV)
Open questions
  • Which NAV do we use, and what if it's stale or not yet published?
  • Do we apply a buffer haircut beyond LTV?
  • Compute at scheme level or portfolio level?
  • Recompute on every page load, or use a cached result?
Pipeline: NAV ingestion → valuation service → loan policy engine → offer engine — kept separate because NAV and policy change at different speeds.
D4Pledge orchestrationproblem 6
Select holdings→Pledge orchestrator creates request→RTA / exchange / depository→Customer OTP / consent→External confirmation→Internal ledger update
Hard questions
  • Is pledge creation idempotent on retry?
  • What if the partner says "accepted" but the callback never arrives?
  • What if the callback arrives twice?
  • What if the internal DB update fails after external success?
Needs: idempotency key, external reference mapping, callback event store, reconciliation job, compensation workflow.
D5Collateral ledgerproblem 7
Kept fully separate from the loan ledger:
customer_idloan_account_idscheme_idfolio_nopledged_unitspledge_referencevaluation_at_pledgecurrent_valuationlien_statusliquidation_status
Why separate?
Audit needs answers to: what units are locked, which pledge created this lien, which units were released, which were liquidated, and what they were worth at grant. Without this ledger, audit becomes painful.
D6Risk & margin monitoringproblem 8
Periodic NAV refresh→Recompute collateral value→Compare to outstanding loan→Zone classification
≤ 70%Healthy
70–75%Warning
75–80%Freeze further drawdown
> 80%Liquidation trigger
Risk is not a cron job.
It needs valuation freshness, threshold rules, alerting, an action workflow, an audit trail, and a link into collections.
D7Liquidation engineproblem 9
Sensitive questions
  • Can liquidation be fully automatic, or does it need human sign-off?
  • Is partial liquidation allowed?
  • Sell cheapest units first, or most liquid first?
  • What if the market is closed, or the partner rejects the redemption?
Liquidation strategy engine — input: shortfall amount, eligible pledged schemes, liquidity priority, tax/fairness rules, ops constraints → output: a redemption plan.
Risk breach→Compute shortfall→Choose units to redeem→Send redemption / enforce→Receive proceeds→Adjust outstanding
Liquidation strategy is a business policy, not just technical logic.
Keep it configurable.
D8LOS vs LMS splitproblem 10
LOS
Loan origination — before disbursal: application, eligibility, offer, documents, pledge initiation, sanction decision.
LMS
Loan management — after activation: account creation, interest accrual, statements, repayment, overdue, foreclosure, collections.
Why split: origination and servicing evolve at different speeds, and LMS is often an external enterprise platform.

F
Resilience & consistency 3 topics
F1Failure scenarios to hunt for5 cases
Stale holdings shown to the customer
A pledge request uses old units while the customer was shown more drawing power than is actually possible.
Pledge confirmed externally, internal state stuck
The collateral is locked externally but the internal record stays pending — the loan is wrongly blocked.
Race condition: disbursed before pledge confirmed
A major control failure — money leaves before the collateral is actually secured.
NAV crashes, margin job runs late
The risk window goes unaddressed for the gap between the crash and the next risk run.
Full repayment, delayed lien release
A terrible customer experience — the debt is gone but the collateral is still legally locked.

G
Mastery layer 4 topics
G1Architect-level talking points6 points
Collateral is units, not money.
The system must preserve unit-level traceability.
Offer generation and pledge confirmation are separated.
Displayed eligibility is preliminary; enforceable eligibility comes only after a successful pledge.
This must be state-machine driven.
External partner calls make the journey long-running and failure-prone.
Risk monitoring is a first-class subsystem.
Not a support job bolted on afterward.
The collateral ledger is not the loan ledger.
Related lifecycles, but not identical ones.
A policy engine controls eligibility, LTV, and liquidation thresholds.
Because these business rules change often, and code deploys shouldn't gate them.
G2Deep-mastery questions4 lenses
Product / business
  • Why would a customer choose this over simply redeeming units?
  • Which customer segments fit this product best?
  • Which schemes create high operational risk but low revenue value?
  • How do we avoid lending against volatile assets at the wrong time?
  • Show only approved holdings, or all holdings with reasons for ineligibility?
Risk
  • Should LTV depend only on scheme type, or also the customer's credit profile?
  • One uniform margin rule, or dynamic risk buckets?
  • What happens if NAV is stale for two days?
  • Can drawdown continue while the warning threshold is breached?
  • Should liquidation be rule-based or maker-checker approved?
Operations
  • How does support see where a journey is stuck?
  • How do we safely replay failed callback-driven steps?
  • What's the reconciliation process with the RTA / pledge partner?
  • How do we safely release the lien after full repayment?
  • Which SLAs matter most — offer generation, pledge marking, disbursal, or lien release?
Architecture
  • Which parts must be synchronous for UX, and which must be async for resilience?
  • What idempotency keys exist across the loan app, pledge request, disbursement, and callbacks?
  • Where do we need transactional guarantees, and where is eventual consistency fine?
  • How do we design auditability for a regulator, dispute, or ops inquiry?
  • Which domain events are contract-worthy and stable enough for other systems to consume?
G3Interview-ready answerone paragraph
I'd design loan against mutual fund as a collateralized lending platform with five core capabilities: holdings discovery, eligibility and valuation, pledge orchestration, loan servicing, and continuous risk monitoring. The key architectural principle is that mutual fund units are the real collateral, so I'd maintain a separate collateral ledger and a state-machine-driven journey. Because external pledge partners and valuation feeds are involved, I'd keep customer-facing eligibility synchronous where possible, but push pledge confirmation, callbacks, reconciliation, and margin workflows into asynchronous orchestration. The hardest part isn't disbursal — it's ongoing collateral risk management and safe liquidation.
