G-Sec on Optimus · Field Notes

The Gilt Edge

Government securities are called gilt-edged instruments — practically default-free, backed by the sovereign. This document is the gilt edge running down everything we know about bringing G-Sec to retail customers on Optimus: the bond mechanics, the systems, the buy journey, the interest math, and every open question still sitting on the table.

It exists so that six months from now — when someone asks "wait, why does the holdings page show that number?" — the answer is in here, not in someone's memory of a meeting.

How this is built

  • Search the top bar for any term — LIP, CCIL, Kafka, STRIPS — and it'll jump you straight to the mention.
  • Every section opens with a question worth holding in your head before you read, closes with bullet takeaways, and ends with a harder question worth sitting with.
  • Diagrams are for revision, not decoration — if you only have two minutes before a meeting, scan the diagrams in a section and you'll have 80% of it back.
  • Open questions live in one place (§10) instead of scattered through every section, so nothing quietly gets forgotten.
Why "gilt edge"? Old British government bond certificates literally had gilt (gold-leafed) edges — a physical signal that this paper was as safe as paper gets. The accent colour running down this whole document is a small nod to that.
Section 01 · Foundations

Bond Fundamentals

Before you dive in

If a bond's quoted price and the amount that actually leaves a customer's account can differ — what's hiding inside that gap, and who decided it should be there?

A bond is just a loan running in reverse: the investor lends, the issuer borrows, and a coupon is the interest on that loan. A Government Security (G-Sec) is the same thing issued by the Centre or a State — and because a sovereign borrower essentially cannot default on its own currency debt, G-Secs are called gilt-edged: risk-free in practice, even though nothing is risk-free in theory.

The instrument family — T-bills, GSecs, SDLs, STRIPS

The Centre issues two shapes of debt; States issue only one:

T-BILL
Money-market, ≤1 year, zero-coupon — sold at a discount, redeemed at face value. No periodic interest at all.
91 / 182 / 364-day tenors
GSEC / DATED SECURITY
Centre-issued, >1 year, coupon-bearing, paid semi-annually.
e.g. 6.79% GS 2034
SGS / SDL
State Development Loan — a State government's version of a dated security. Same pricing mechanics as a GSec.
Govt of Karnataka, Govt of Maharashtra…
C-STRIPS / P-STRIPS
A coupon bond's cashflows separated into individually tradeable zero-coupon pieces — one STRIP per coupon, one for the final principal.
Up to 50-year maturities

On the Optimus front end, the display name is derived from Category + Issuer, not stored verbatim — and it's worth knowing the rule, because it explains why two very different instruments can look almost identical in a list:

TBILLGovt of IndiaT-Bill-Govt. of India
GSECGovt of IndiaGovt. of India
SGSGovt of KarnatakaGovt of Karnataka

Yield, price, and the number that actually gets debited

Yield to Maturity (YTM) is the single discount rate that makes the present value of every future cashflow equal today's market price — found by trial and error, or instantly via Excel's YIELD() function. Flip the question around and Excel's PRICE() function gives you price from a known yield.

Day-count convention decides how "holding period" gets measured, and India runs two different ones depending on the instrument:

  • Bond market — 30/360: every month is treated as exactly 30 days, every year as 360. Used for GSecs, SDLs, and STRIPS.
  • Money market — actual/365: real calendar days over 365. Used for T-bills.

Mixing these up anywhere in a pricing or accrual calculation produces a wrong-but-plausible-looking number — exactly the kind of bug that survives QA and shows up as a customer complaint instead.

And the quoted "clean price" is never what's actually paid. The buyer also pays accrued interest — the coupon the seller has earned but not yet received for the days they held the bond since the last coupon date. Clean price + accrued interest = dirty price = total consideration — and the dirty price, not the clean price, is the number that should hit the debit.

What this tells you
  • Centre issues T-bills (zero-coupon, discount-priced) and dated securities (coupon-bearing); States issue only dated securities, called SDLs/SGS.
  • STRIPS split a coupon bond's cashflows into individually tradeable zero-coupon pieces — but Optimus's FE name doesn't distinguish Coupon-STRIPS from Principal-STRIPS.
  • Two day-count conventions coexist in this one product: 30/360 for bonds, actual/365 for T-bills. Every pricing or accrual function needs to know which instrument it's looking at.
  • What gets debited is the dirty price (clean price + accrued interest), not the quoted clean price.
Sit with this

If a dated G-Sec's accrued-interest calculation was accidentally implemented with actual/365 instead of 30/360, would the number come out too high or too low — and on which calendar dates would the error be largest?

February is the giveaway month: 30/360 always counts it as 30 days, actual/365 counts it as 28 (or 29). The error direction flips depending on which side of February the holding period falls — which is exactly the kind of bug that only shows up a few times a year and is hard to catch in testing.
Section 02 · The Map

System Architecture

Before you dive in

When a customer never even opens the G-Sec tab, how many backend systems should that customer be silently costing you?

Optimus and Salesforce form the core of everything: Optimus is the customer-facing app, Salesforce orchestrates SRs (Service Requests) for onboarding, inventory, and buy flows. Everything else hangs off that core.

FIG 2.1 — Bank platform and market infrastructure
What each system actually does

CDP holds CCID identity linked to UCIC — the "is this person already known to us" lookup. eDMS stores AOF forms and deal PDFs. FM Converge books deals and is the system of record for invested amount and units held. Winsoft feeds G-Sec into the broader stock listing. CCIL runs NDS-OM, the actual clearing market. NSDL records settlement obligations; Speed-e is NSDL's tool that executes the depository transfer.

What this tells you
  • SFDC↔Optimus talks P2P; everything reaching third parties or FM Converge goes through Kong.
  • FM Converge — not SFDC — is the source of truth for the actual debited amount and units held.
  • Business/Ops still does literal manual file uploads into CCIL and Speed-e. Not everything here is automated yet.
Sit with this

If FM Converge went down for an hour in the middle of trading, which Optimus screens would start showing stale or wrong numbers — and why specifically those, and not the others?

Bond Details' invested amount, units held, and avg price all source directly from FM Converge, so a fresh buy wouldn't have a real number to show — likely blank or stuck on a stale value. Holdings would lag the same way for anything bought during the outage. SFDC-sourced fields (upcoming interest date, SR status, the bond catalog itself) would carry on fine, since they don't depend on FM Converge at all. The buy journey breaks harder than the display layer does: deal booking explicitly routes through FM Converge with its own 3-retry budget, so new buys during the outage burn through retries and fall to manual ops handling, while existing holdings just look mildly stale rather than actually broken.
Section 03 · Getting In

Onboarding Journey

Before you dive in

Should "not yet onboarded" and "permanently rejected" really be the same branch in this flow?

Before a customer can buy a single G-Sec unit, the system has to answer one question: is this person already registered with CCIL? Three quick checks decide that, and any one of them being "yes" short-circuits straight to "already linked."

FIG 3.1 — CCIL registration gate
Why these three checks, in this order

CDP comes first because it's the cheapest lookup — an already-known customer skips everything else immediately. SR status comes next because it catches anyone mid-flow without having to re-ask CCIL anything. The cross-partner check runs last, which makes sense given it's the most expensive call (a real round trip via Kong) — and is probably no coincidence that it's also the one still flagged unconfirmed in the original sketch.

What this tells you
  • The three checks: CCIL ID already in CDP, SR status, and cross-partner CCIL existence (checked via Kong). Any "yes" shortcuts to "already linked."
  • The "CCIL exists with another partner" check was flagged red in the original sketch — treat it as unconfirmed, not settled logic.
  • Final registration needs both a valid demat account and a valid savings account before SFDC raises the SR.
  • The branch is labelled WIP, not a clean Yes/No — worth confirming there isn't a missing hard-rejection state.
Sit with this

A customer who's mid-registration — SR raised, not yet uploaded to CCIL — opens the app again. Which of the three early checks catches them, and what should they actually see on screen?

The "CCIL ID in CDP" check would say no — nothing's been uploaded to CCIL yet, so that one doesn't catch them. The "SR status" check is the one that should: there's an open, non-terminal SR sitting there, which is exactly what this check exists to catch. The cross-partner check is irrelevant here, since it's about CCIL IDs registered elsewhere, not this customer's own pending one. They should probably see the same "application in progress" message used for the WIP branch — though it's worth confirming the SR-status check actually distinguishes "SR raised, nothing's happened yet" from "uploaded to CCIL, awaiting confirmation," since those are different failure points that probably deserve different messaging.
Section 04 · No Spinners

Loader-Free Permissions

Before you dive in

Is it cheaper to ask four questions for every single user on every app launch, or to ask zero questions and risk being wrong once in a while?

The goal: a customer should never see a loading spinner when they tap into G-Sec. That means deciding in advance whether they can buy, view, or need onboarding — and the only way to do that is to resolve four checks before the customer even asks.

FIG 4.1 — Permission fan-out on app launch

Three ways to pay for this

Approach 1 — fetch everything live, on every app launch

Permissions are always ready, regardless of entry point. The cost: every single launch fires calls to Grade service and the SFDC case-inquiry API, even for the vast majority of users who never go near G-Sec.

Approach 2 — same idea, but cache SFDC case status client-side on Optimus

Removes the per-launch SFDC call entirely. The cost: correctness now depends on SFDC reliably emitting case-status-change events. A missed event leaves the cache wrong, with no built-in way to notice.

Alternate — bulk SR-status pull, cached for ~1 day

Sidesteps the "missed event" fragility entirely by pulling on a schedule instead of waiting for a push. The cost shifts from reliability to staleness — a status could be up to a day old.

What this tells you
  • Four checks decide buy/view vs. onboarding-redirect: the feature toggle, CCIL-onboarded flag, active-SR-in-SFDC, and a visit-count nudge from Grade/Mongo.
  • Every option here trades the same two things against each other: freshness and reliability. Push gives freshness but is fragile to missed events; pull gives reliability but bounds freshness to the polling interval.
  • This is the exact same tension as the Kafka delta-sync design in §5 — worth deciding the push-vs-pull philosophy once, deliberately, rather than re-litigating it per feature.
Sit with this

If you went with Approach 2 and SFDC silently failed to emit one case-closed event, how would you even detect that the cache had drifted — before a customer complained about it?

There's no built-in detector in the design as described — that's the risk being flagged. Honest options: (a) a periodic reconciliation job that spot-checks a sample of cached statuses against SFDC directly, cheap enough not to defeat the point of caching; (b) a TTL on the cache regardless of events, so a missed event self-corrects within a bounded window — this quietly turns Approach 2 into a hybrid with the pull-based Alternate; or (c) no detector at all, and customer complaints become the detection mechanism by default. Worth deciding which of these on purpose, rather than discovering the answer is "(c)" by accident.

Before you dive in

The five events that can change bond data, one at a time

The 2-hour tick (or faster in volatile markets); a toggle disable/enable event; a delta-only payload describing just the change, never the whole record; deletion of a bond record entirely; and a change to the bond's field-level structure itself. Five genuinely different triggers — worth treating as five distinct cases on the Optimus side rather than one undifferentiated "inventory changed" event.

What this tells you
  • ~150–250 bonds total, ~100 quantity-changing cases a day — small absolute numbers, but every one of them has to survive a delta-only pipe correctly.
  • Quantity below a set threshold is hidden on the FE regardless of the manual toggle — two independent ways for a bond to disappear from view.
  • Ownership: Kafka/pub-sub connectivity — Ashish / Roshani. Optimus-side display + the toggle — Sreejanya.
Sit with this

A bond's quantity drops below the display threshold mid-day, outside the normal 2-hour cycle. Does the delta-only design actually guarantee Optimus finds out in time — or only that it'll find out eventually?

Only eventually, on the evidence we have. The 2-hour cadence is the normal tick, with "if market is volatile" as the only stated reason it runs faster — nothing says crossing the display threshold itself forces an earlier sync. So if a bond sells out at 10:05am right after a 10:00am sync, Optimus's displayed inventory could be wrong for nearly two hours, during which a customer could place an order against quantity that no longer exists — which loops straight back into the "order quantity exceeds actual available quantity" open question in the buy journey.

Section 07 · Doing The Math

Pricing, Naming & Cashflow Logic

Before you dive in

If no system anywhere stores an actual interest payment, what exactly is the "interest earned" number on your screen computed from?

Pricing sheet quirks: tenure, total interest, and the 29th/30th/31st problem

Tenure buckets (ultra-short / short / medium / long) are driven off residual maturity — confirmed inclusive of both today's date and the maturity date. The pricing sheet's "total interest per unit" feeds the buy-details page: total interest payout = total interest per unit × units entered.

A real edge case, confirmed to actually happen: coupon dates can fall on the 29th, 30th, or 31st of a month — a day the 30/360 convention was never built to represent cleanly. The cashflow logic has to special-case it, and it needs two specific parameters from SFDC to do so (not yet specified).

The proposed payout-schedule algorithm

Roll the bond's LIP (last interest payment date) and NIP (next interest payment date) forward by exactly one year at a time, repeatedly, until you reach maturity. That generates the full coupon schedule from two stored dates.

FIG 7.1 — Rolling LIP/NIP forward, and where it breaks
Catalog naming, recapped — and why it matters at scale

Display name on Optimus is derived from Category + Issuer (see §1 for the full table). The thing worth remembering: C-STRIPS and P-STRIPS show identically — "STRIPS - {Issuer}" — even though their cashflow profiles are completely different (one is a coupon piece, one is the principal). If that distinction ever needs to surface anywhere on the buy or details flow, it isn't coming from the catalog name.

What this tells you
  • There is no system of record for actual interest or redemption payments anywhere in this stack. Every interest figure shown anywhere in the product is computed live from bond master fields — there's no ledger to check it against.
  • The LIP/NIP rolling-forward logic is therefore not a convenience calculation — it is the system of record, by default, whether or not that was the intention.
  • A blank LIP on any G-Sec/SDL breaks the chain completely, with no fallback.
  • Two unspecified SFDC parameters are needed just to handle the 29th/30th/31st coupon-date edge case.
Sit with this

Given there's no payments ledger to reconcile against, how would you even notice if the LIP/NIP rolling logic silently drifted wrong for one bond, over several coupon cycles, with nobody watching?

Same shape of answer as the permissions question above: without a ledger, detection has to be built on purpose. Candidates: cross-check the LIP/NIP-derived schedule against the pricing sheet's flat "total interest per unit" for the same bond (the redundancy flagged in §10 is actually useful here, not just a risk); periodically spot-audit a sample of bonds' computed coupon dates against the SFDC bond master; or add a sanity bound — a computed coupon period of 0 days or more than ~190 days is almost certainly LIP or NIP rolling wrong. None of these are a stated plan yet, just cheap relative to being silently wrong.
Section 08 · The Subtle Part

Interest Accrual: Paid vs. Earned

Before you dive in

When you buy a bond mid-period, are you being charged interest you don't deserve — or pre-paying for interest you're about to earn back?

It's the second one. Walk through it with a real example: Customer B buys a bond mid-period from Customer A, holds it across one full coupon period, then sells it mid-period to someone else.

FIG 8.1 — How one bond's coupons split across two ownership changes
Walking through the numbers

Reading left to right: ₹1 and ₹3 split IP1 between Customer A (held Jan–buy date) and Customer B (buy date–Jul 2024). IP2's full ₹4 belongs entirely to B since no resale happened that period. ₹2 and ₹2 split IP3 between B and the next buyer.

B's true economic interest = AI(2)+AI(3)+AI(4) = 3+4+2 = ₹9. But B also paid AI(1)=₹1 to Customer A at purchase — and that gets folded into both her investment amount and her displayed interest earned, making displayed earned = AI(1)+AI(2)+AI(3)+AI(4) = 1+3+4+2 = ₹10. (Worth a quick sanity check against the original notes, which wrote this total as ₹9 — likely a typo, but worth confirming since an off-by-one-coupon error at scale matters.)

Why Holdings and Bond Details show different numbers — on purpose
FieldLives onBehaviour
Interest earnedHoldings pageCumulative since purchase, includes the AI(1) paid-to-seller pass-through, grows daily, never resets.
Interest accumulatedBond Details pageBefore first coupon: paid-to-seller + earned-till-today. After: earned-till-today only — resets to ~0 right after every payout.
Interest receivedBond Details pageCumulative sum of every coupon actually paid out so far (IP1+IP2+...).

Bond Details' (accumulated + received) should always equal Holdings' single "interest earned" figure at any given moment — they're two different slices of the same economic total, not two competing answers.

Constraint worth remembering: upcoming interest dates come from SFDC as-is. SFDC confirmed it cannot compute the holiday-adjusted actual payment date — so Optimus just shows the nominal date with a disclaimer icon, and only surfaces it 1–2 months before payment.
What this tells you
  • Buying mid-period means pre-paying the seller's accrued interest — and getting that exact amount back at the next coupon.
  • "Interest earned" (Holdings) and "interest accumulated" (Bond Details) are deliberately different numbers serving different screens, not a bug.
  • "Interest received" is a running lifetime total across every coupon paid so far — never resets.
Sit with this

At the exact moment a coupon is paid, do Holdings' "interest earned" and Bond Details' ("interest accumulated" + "interest received") actually show the same total — and what would it mean for the product if they ever didn't?

They should, by construction — Holdings' cumulative "interest earned" is meant to equal what Bond Details splits into "accumulated" (current period) and "received" (all prior periods). A divergence would mean either: the two figures are computed from different source events and have drifted apart, or the "interest accumulated" reset is firing at a slightly different moment than the "interest received" bump — e.g. a timezone or end-of-day cutoff mismatch between the two calculations. Either way, this is one of the easier internal-consistency checks to build into a test suite, since the two numbers are supposed to be mathematically related, not independently observed facts.

What happens at maturity

Sit with this

A bond matures and drops off Holdings. Six months later, a customer taps into an old transaction for it on the Transactions page. What should that page be able to show them, given the bond's own details record no longer exists anywhere?

At minimum, the transaction record needs to be self-contained — ISIN, bond name, quantity, price, and date can't depend on a live lookup to a Bond Details record that's already gone. That likely means Transactions needs to snapshot the relevant bond details at the time of each transaction, rather than joining live against current bond master data. The harder question hiding in here: does the customer expect to see the bond's final outcome — last interest payment, maturity payout — on this page too, since Bond Details won't show it anymore? If yes, Transactions is being asked to do more than list historical buys and sells; it becomes the only place the maturity outcome is visible at all.
